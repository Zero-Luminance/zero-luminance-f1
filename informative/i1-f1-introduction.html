Introduction To Formula 1

By Zero Luminance | 22/02/2022

Formula One (F1) is a single-seater open wheel international racing championship regulated by the Fédération Internationale de l’Automobile (FIA) & is considered the pinnacle of motorsport. ‘Formula’ refers to the FIA created rules which all participants must adhere to & 'One' indicates the sport being the highest tier of racing.

The sport may accommodate a maximum of 13 teams (a.k.a Constructors) of which, employ 2 drivers who are super license qualified; as of the 2022 FIA Formula 1 World Championship(1) there exists 10 teams & thus 20 drivers.

A Formula 1 Championship consists of Grand Prix (i.e. races) hosted globally on a track/circuit; F1 has historically conducted between 7-22 grand prix a year. Each Grand Prix presents the opportunity for teams & drivers to score points which contribute to their standings in the championship.

The F1 Championship consists of 2 categories: the Constructors & Drivers Championship. The Constructor’s (or Team) Championship consists of the points accrued by BOTH the team’s drivers over race weekends. The Driver’s Championship stacks drivers against each other individually regardless of team. Obviously, the team or driver with the most points wins the respective championship.

The Appeal Of Formula 1 Cars & Drivers:

What separates F1 cars from any other is its ability to create a large volume of aerodynamic downforce & thus be capable of blisteringly fast cornering speeds. These machines are no easy feat to operate too as a large number of driver aids found in many cars such as automatic shifting, traction control, active suspension, etc.

The consequence of driving F1 cars quickly through corners is the G-Force exerted on their body. G-Forces peak at around 6.5 lateral g (6 times the F1 driver’s body weight). Drivers experience such forces any instance they turn, accelerate or decelerate. When combining how hot the driver’s cockpit can be (usually at least 44C)(2) & the forces are they endure, a total of 2 - 4.5kg(3) of weight is lost per race (2 hours). F1 drivers do not just turn & touch the throttle or break peddles, they are athletes.

To clarify the amount of money poured into these cars, Ferrari back in 2018 spent $512.69 million (usd) on research & development(4) & still LOST to Mercedes AMG in both the constructors & drivers championship with three races left to spare. Hundreds of millions of dollars are spent mainly on a car’s aerodynamic body, engine, breaks, etc all to have a competitive edge amounting to hundredths or thousandths in lap time. Losing hundredths of a second per lap may seem insignificant to the ordinary person, but hundredths may add up to several seconds over the course of a race which is considered an eternity in Formula 1.

An International Circus:

As a global sport, Formula 1 conducts it’s Grand Prix on FIA-graded race circuits around the world. Each country typically holds accommodates 1 Grand Prix, e.g. Italian in Monza, Australian Grand Prix in Albert Park, etc. Historically the 1950-60s would accommodate 11 Grand Prix yearly, with the 1970s-2000s holding 14-17 races. Ever since Liberty Media bought the Formula 1 brand in late 2016, races in 2021 & 2022 have reached a record high of 22 yearly.

Each Grand Prix (GP) circuit has defining characteristics which in turn challenges engineers & drivers in different ways. The Singaporean GP was the first ever night race in F1, one of few true-street circuits & is known for its hot & humid weather easily making it the circuit that driver’s lose the most amount of weight due to excessive sweating. The Mexican GP circuit has the highest geographical altitude from sea level; the air is more thin which in turn forces engineers to account for the decrease in downforce a car will have to command.

Thus for people with the travel-bug, Formula 1 serves as the ultimate excuse to go & see different countries. As even after the race weekend ends there exists a life outside the hectic motorsport circus. There is culture, food & venues travellers will be able to experience.
